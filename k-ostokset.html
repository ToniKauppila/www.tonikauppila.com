K-Ostokset

K-Ostokset is an award winning data service for K-groups loyalty program customers where users can see their households detailed grocery shopping data, domesticity rate and carbon footprint from the past 12 months. The service provides users with the ability to set goals and gives suggestions and tips to make grocery shopping more sustainable.

Backstory

K-group has about 3 600 000 K-Plussa loyalty customers. The loyalty program is a big source of detailed data, which has generally been held in the companys vast data bases and used for analytics and targeted marketing.

In the end of the year 2018, came the time to make the data visible for the loyalty customers and set the foundation for K-Ostokset, a data service for K-Plussa customers.

What started as a innovation challenge won by 8-bit-sheep, turned out to be a successful service and a very interesting journey for me as a designer.

From early sketches to over 40 usability tests, high fidelity prototypes, countles iterations and many features, K-Ostokset has reached over 150 000 users and a Grand One trophy from Best use of data -category.

The team consist of top talents including the inspiring Product Owner Hanna-Reetta Luukkainen and the amazing dev team from 8-bit-sheep, Reaktor and Futurice.

Problems

Me and the team have had many fasinating problems to solve, including:

  • How might we understandably visualize multi-layered data sets?
  • How might we get the service to seamlessly integrate with both a desktop website and a native mobile application using webview?
  • How might we make a strong authentication as frictionless as possible?
  • How might we set up a intuitive navigation for data that is layered in many categories and sub categories?
  • How might we show the domesticity rate of ones purchases?
  • How might we make sense of the environmental effects of peoples grocery purchases?
  • How might we thrive people to consume in a more sustainable way?
  • How might we fusion households data and visualize it in the service?

Research methods

We have tested and gathered insight from over 8 000 customers, including 50 usability tests, two big online surveys, couple of focus groups and numerous in-house tests.

At the beginning we did usability tests with similar service before we even started to build our own prototype. This approach gave us important intel on what people are mainly interested about regarding their own grocery purchase data.

After the creation of our own prototype, initial back and front end, we tested the service with our real, pretty crude version of the coming product to really see the users reaction when the data was real.

At later stages we have tested the new features with our most important target groups via one-to-one usability tests, focus groups and online tests with Maze.

The usual set up

In usability tests, we usually have a set up of two rooms. The other room is the one where one of us interviews the users. The other one is a observatory, where other team members write notes and observe the test via streaming laptop cameras and microphones. At the end of the day we wrap up and try to find patterns that occur from the interviews.

The design process

I'm (obviuosly) a big fan of iterative design, where I'm not the only one who takes part in research and consepting. I think it's important to get the whole team to take part in the beginning and get all the bright minds to work on the complex problems.

The main direction for the design should always come from validations and data - not from peoples own personal preferences.

Here is a simplified break up from the design process of the climate meter and carbon footprint to get some idea of how I usually work:

  1. Gained insight on how people percieve and understand carbon footprint, carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) and if they are interested on seeing their own carbon footprint from their grocery purchases. Used tools: online survey and six one-hour user interviews including usability test with a similar service.
  2. Studied the structure of the carbon footprint data we would get regarding grocery purchases.
  3. Sketched ideas and iterated with the team.
  4. Decided on the structure, colors, graphs and copys and tested them via Maze and InVision prototype.
  5. Iterated the concept again.
  6. Created implementation strategy with the product owner and developers.
  7. Supported the developers with pixels and solved various problems together.
  8. Launched as soon as we could and went through insight from analytics, customer feedback, social media, collegues, friends and family.
  9. Iterated again and solved problems that rose from insights and enhanced the feature.

The carbon footprint feature was a very intersting design task to work with. Sustainability and ecologial values are close to my heart so it was really rewarding and motivating to design stuff that potentially has a big impact on peoples perception about their shopping habits and eating.

Features

The most important features that I have designed so far have been:

  1. Basic site map, vision and the visual identity for the whole service.
  2. Login flow and introduction including strong authentication.
  3. Charts and tables for showing the different time selections and categories.
  4. Domesticy meter, product recommendations and goal setting.
  5. Mobile app integration that uses webview technology
  6. Ability to add household member’s data to the service.
  7. Climate meter and carbon footprint data visualization with tips and goal setting.

We’ve made all the desicions to implement features as a team, with the help of customer interviews, surveys and feedback.
